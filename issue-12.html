One · Markets

The 30-year Treasury just cracked 5.1%

The 30-year Treasury yield closed at 5.10% on Thursday — the highest level in nearly a year, and the first time the long end has sustained above five since the early-Volcker era. The whole curve moved with it: the 10-year climbed to 4.57%, the two-year sits at 4.07%. The flattening curve everyone watched for in Q1 has steepened back out, and not in the direction investors expected.

Why this matters. The 30-year is the benchmark behind every mortgage, every corporate bond, and every state highway financed with municipal debt — when it climbs, the cost of building anything climbs with it. But the drivers here aren’t market mood; they’re structural. Fiscal year 2026 is on track to add another $1.8 trillion to a debt already past $35 trillion, the 2025 reconciliation bill extended the TCJA cuts without offsetting revenue, and a Fed governor’s resignation this month put Fed independence back in the political conversation. Foreign Treasury demand has softened in parallel — Japan and China have been slowly reducing holdings for six quarters. Investors are demanding more yield to hold US debt because the political path for fixing the debt trajectory has narrowed.

What this means for you. If you’re locking a mortgage this summer, today’s math is meaningfully different from February’s — the cuts you priced in won’t fully arrive. Buyers refinancing into a 30-year fixed will see higher monthly payments than rate-cut bulls were modeling. On the other side: cash sitting in money-market funds is earning real yields above inflation for the first time in years, and that quietly ends the moment the Fed actually does cut. The window to lock in 5%-plus on cash is open now and won’t stay open forever.

Sources: reuters.com · treasury.gov · bloomberg.com

Two · Business

Ben & Jerry’s parent is quietly dismantling what made the brand worth a billion

Magnum Ice Cream Company — the standalone business Unilever spun off in late 2025 — pulled $1.3 billion in revenue from Ben & Jerry’s alone last quarter. The company controls 21% of the global ice cream market and is the world’s largest pure-play in the category. Its stock is still down 25% from its February high, despite four straight quarters of earnings beats.

Why this matters. Unilever paid $326 million for Ben & Jerry’s in 2000 for one reason: the brand’s values posture — the cause campaigns, the press releases on Israel and racial justice, the fact that Cherry Garcia and political commentary came in the same pint. The retreat happening now isn’t ideological, it’s directed. Trian Partners pushed Unilever for the spin-off in 2024 explicitly to maximize ice cream margins. Magnum’s new CEO was appointed with an explicit depoliticize mandate. And red-state attorneys general have been actively pressuring corporate political speech since the 2024 Bud Light cycle that scared every CMO in America. Ben & Jerry’s is now running the same playbook as Disney, Target, and Anheuser-Busch — a quiet retreat from cause-attached marketing, framed internally as “returning to fundamentals.”

What this means for you. If you bought Ben & Jerry’s specifically because of its politics, the calculus is changing — watch the next product launch for which causes they choose to comment on, or skip. The bigger story: every consumer brand carrying a values-attached premium is running this playbook right now. The 25-30% you pay for “company that shares my values” coffee, athletic wear, food, beauty — that premium is being repriced down across the consumer landscape over the next 18 months. Either the price-conscious shopper notices the savings, or the activist shopper notices the retreat and the brand loses both ways at once.

Sources: ft.com · bloomberg.com · wsj.com

Three · Science

MIT just lost a fifth of its incoming graduate class. The bill comes due in 2036.

Incoming graduate enrollment at MIT dropped 20% this year — about 500 fewer students. The cuts cluster in physical sciences and engineering, the exact disciplines that produce most of America’s R&D pipeline. Federal research funding to MIT is down more than 20% over the same window. The endowment excise tax was raised in the 2025 reconciliation bill from 1.4% to 8% of investment gains, costing the school hundreds of millions a year.

Why this matters. Graduate students do most of the experimental work that turns into patents — about half of US patents come out of academia, and MIT files more than any other school. The drivers here are entirely policy, not market. The 2025 reconciliation bill explicitly targeted elite research universities with the endowment tax hike, and Harvard, Yale, Princeton, and Stanford were all hit the same way. The FY26 continuing resolution capped NIH/NSF/DOE grant ceilings. F-1 and J-1 visa caps cut international STEM grad enrollment 30% at top programs. And the broader political framing — that elite universities are politically opposed and should be defunded — has shifted what universities can plan for. This isn’t accidental fiscal tightening. It’s directed.

What this means for you. If your kid is heading into STEM grad school, the next two admission cycles will be unusually competitive — fewer slots, more applicants per slot. The funding picture for first-year postdocs has gotten meaningfully harder; expect more international PhDs choosing Europe or Canada over the US. If you work in tech and depend on US-trained PhDs to hire, that pipeline tightens through 2028 — the most expensive STEM labor market in the world, already squeezed, gets squeezed harder. And in policy work: the productivity hit shows up in TFP measurements 8-12 years from now, exactly when policymakers will be wondering why growth keeps disappointing.

Sources: mit.edu · insidehighered.com · chronicle.com
